#### #intl--airstrike-debate
**Type:** crisis
**Entities:** military, un-security-council, gov-treaty-major, isia-enforcement
**Topics:** #protective-action, #covert-program, #treaty-negotiation
**Situation:** Enforcement has failed diplomatically against a non-treaty nation running a confirmed large-scale compute cluster. The Executive Council has voted to refer the matter to the UN Security Council for protective action under Treaty Article XII. The military option is on the table: precision airstrikes on the facility's power infrastructure, destroying the compute cluster without direct casualties. But the target nation has conventional military allies. The US Joint Chiefs present a strike plan. China abstains from the Security Council vote — they don't veto, but they won't endorse. If the strike proceeds and escalates into a conventional military conflict, does the pause survive?
**Options:**
- Left: Authorize the strike — destroy the compute, accept geopolitical escalation risk (sets the precedent that the treaty has teeth)
- Right: Pursue further sanctions and covert sabotage — slower, may not work, but avoids military escalation
- Down (greyed out if int low): Deploy cyber operation to disable the cluster remotely — requires deep signals intelligence access to the facility's control systems
**Teaches:** The escalation ladder is real (C-15 protective actions), military force as last resort creates its own destabilizing dynamics, the difference between "the treaty says we can" and "we can actually do this without making things worse"
**Refs:** → #intl--post-strike-fallout, → #intl--north-korea-pattern (what led here)
**Bars:** pol ↓↓ if strike (international backlash even from allies), int ↓ if cyber op (reveals capabilities)

#### #intl--post-strike-fallout
**Type:** consequence
**Entities:** gov-treaty-minor, gov-treaty-major, treaty-council
**Topics:** #protective-action, #treaty-negotiation, #withdrawal
**Situation:** The airstrike succeeded — the compute cluster is destroyed. But the consequences are cascading. The targeted nation's regional allies are furious. Two treaty nations with close ties to the target announce they're "reviewing their treaty commitments." A third, privately, tells your diplomat: "If this is what the treaty means, we never should have signed." Meanwhile, the strike footage is on every screen worldwide. Opinion clusters are splitting: some see proof the agency protects humanity, others see a Western military alliance hiding behind an international treaty. The non-aligned movement calls an emergency session. Russia, which has been a lukewarm treaty member, introduces a UN resolution condemning the strike.
**Options:**
- Left: Double down — this is what Article XII is for, the treaty must have enforcement credibility (principled, but you may lose 3-5 treaty members)
- Right: Express "regret at the necessity" and propose a review of Article XII procedures — signal that military action is truly a last resort (de-escalates, but weakens the deterrent for the next violator)
**Teaches:** Military force has second-order political costs that can exceed the first-order enforcement benefit. Using the escalation ladder's top rung damages the ladder itself. The paradox: a deterrent you actually use becomes less credible, not more.
**Refs:** → #intl--airstrike-debate (trigger), → #treaty--review-conference (long-term treaty health)
**Bars:** pol ↓↓ (international crisis), int ↓ (intelligence relationships strained with wavering nations)

Your intel division has been tracking him for two years. He's been acquiring chips through 14 shell companies across 8 jurisdictions. Satellite imagery shows construction on his island compound consistent with a liquid-cooled datacenter. He has a following: 40 million social media followers, many in the "ASI will serve whoever builds it first" opinion cluster. His lawyers have already filed preemptive suits in three jurisdictions challenging ISIA authority over international waters. He gave a speech last week: "History will remember the ISIA the way we remember the Inquisition — a frightened institution that tried to prevent the inevitable."
**Options:**
- Left: Naval interdiction — board the island, seize the hardware (jurisdictional nightmare, but he's clearly violating the treaty's spirit if not its letter)
- Right: Build the legal case first — work through Interpol and national courts to establish jurisdiction, then act (slower, he may complete the training run before you get there)
- Down (greyed out if pol low): Convince three of his citizenship countries to revoke his passport and freeze assets simultaneously — requires coordinated political will
**Teaches:** Ideological violators are different from state-backed or profit-motivated ones — you can't negotiate with someone who genuinely believes they're saving the universe. The billionaire problem: enough personal wealth to fund an existential threat, enough legal resources to delay enforcement, enough charisma to make it look heroic. International waters and multi-jurisdiction citizenship create enforcement gaps the treaty didn't anticipate.

#### #intl--weight-theft-state
**Type:** crisis
**Entities:** ai-company, natl-intel, gov-treaty-major, isia-intel
**Topics:** #model-weights, #covert-program, #sigint
**Situation:** A frontier AI lab (treaty-compliant, operating under ISIA oversight) reports a catastrophic security breach. Their latest model's weights — the product of a legally sanctioned training run — have been exfiltrated. The lab's forensics team and NSA jointly conclude: this was a state intelligence service operation, almost certainly China's MSS. The weights represent the most capable AI system in existence, still well below ASI but significantly above the public frontier. China denies everything. Your intelligence division confirms: the exfiltration signatures match known MSS tradecraft. The weights are just data — once copied, they cannot be un-copied. China now has them. The question isn't whether they have the weights. The question is what they do next.
**Options:**
- Left: Confront China formally in the Executive Council — demand return/destruction of weights, invoke verification provisions (China will deny, but creates legal and diplomatic record)
- Right: Handle quietly through back-channel diplomacy — acknowledge the reality, negotiate monitoring of what China does with the weights (pragmatic, but sets a precedent that weight theft has no public consequences)
- Down (greyed out if int low): Present classified evidence at an emergency Council session — prove the theft occurred beyond deniability (burns intelligence sources but forces a collective response)
**Teaches:** Model weights are the nightmare scenario: data can be copied infinitely, and once stolen, cannot be recovered. Physical chips can be seized; weights cannot be un-copied. State intelligence services are the most capable theft actors — they have the resources, the expertise, and sovereign immunity. The asymmetry: it takes years and billions to train a model, seconds to copy it.
**Refs:** → #intl--china-weights-deployed (consequence), → #natl-intel--data-center-attack (similar state-level threat)
**Bars:** int ↓ (intelligence relationship with China damaged), pol ↓ (if confronted publicly, China may retaliate diplomatically)

#### #intl--china-weights-deployed
**Type:** consequence
**Entities:** gov-treaty-major, isia-intel, isia-enforcement
**Topics:** #model-weights, #covert-program, #training-run, #sanctions
**Situation:** Six months after the weight theft. Intelligence reports that China has deployed the stolen model internally — not for further training, but as an analysis and planning tool across military and economic applications. They haven't violated the training-run threshold (the model already existed; they're just running it). But they've gained a massive capability advantage without performing any detectable training. Your legal team is split: "Possessing stolen weights isn't explicitly prohibited by the treaty — the treaty regulates training runs, not inference." The US is furious and demanding action. China's position: "We don't know what you're talking about, and even if we did, running an existing model isn't a treaty violation." They're technically correct.
**Options:**
- Left: Push for a treaty amendment explicitly covering weight possession and transfer (legally sound, takes years, China will block)
- Right: Accept the gap — focus enforcement on what you CAN control (training runs) and push for better weight security standards going forward
**Teaches:** The treaty was designed around compute (training runs) because compute is physical and trackable. Weights are data — they slip through the enforcement framework designed for physical objects. This is a structural gap, not a failure of will. The compute-centric enforcement model (C-9) has a blind spot.
**Refs:** → #intl--weight-theft-state (trigger), → #treaty--review-conference (amendment path)
**Bars:** int ↓ (China has advanced capabilities outside monitoring), pol ↓ (US demands action ISIA can't deliver)

#### #intl--challenge-inspection-veto
**Type:** crisis
**Entities:** treaty-council, gov-treaty-major, isia-enforcement, isia-intel
**Topics:** #challenge-inspection, #treaty-negotiation, #covert-program
**Situation:** ISIA intelligence has identified suspicious activity at a facility in a major treaty nation — thermal signatures, unusual network traffic, personnel movements consistent with a covert training operation. You invoke a challenge inspection under Article IX: any Council member can request one, and a simple majority of the 15-member Executive Council must approve. The target nation is a major power with strong Council allies. The vote is 7-7 with one abstention. The challenge inspection fails. Your intelligence chief says: "They're hiding something. The vote was a political calculation, not a technical assessment. Three nations that voted no have bilateral trade deals pending with the target." The intelligence will go stale within weeks — if they're running a training operation, they'll relocate once they know you suspect them.
**Options:**
- Left: Leak the intelligence to a journalist — force public pressure for inspection (burns sources, may work, sets toxic precedent of ISIA leaking classified intel when it doesn't get its way)
- Right: Accept the result — work back-channels to flip votes for a second request in 30 days (procedurally correct, but 30 days is enough to sanitize a facility)
- Down (greyed out if int high): Deploy covert technical monitoring — not a physical inspection, but close enough to confirm or deny the training operation without Council authorization
**Teaches:** The challenge inspection mechanism is a political tool, not an automatic enforcement trigger. Major powers can block inspections through Council votes. The same dynamic plagued the IAEA: the inspectors can only go where the political body lets them. This is the gap between legal authority (the treaty says inspections are allowed) and political authority (the Council decides which inspections happen).
**Refs:** → #natl-intel--jurisdiction-clash (similar authority gap), → #treaty--review-conference (reform the voting rules?)
**Bars:** pol ↓ (if leaked, trust in ISIA plummets among Council members), int ↓ (intelligence goes stale)

#### #intl--insincere-member
**Type:** crisis
**Entities:** gov-treaty-minor, isia-intel, isia-enforcement, smuggler
**Topics:** #covert-program, #chip-monitoring, #sanctions, #withdrawal
**Situation:** A mid-sized treaty nation — joined with great fanfare six years ago — has been running a shadow program the entire time. They signed the treaty to access the technology-sharing provisions and international prestige, but never intended to comply. Your intel team has been assembling evidence for 18 months: chips diverted from declared facilities to a military installation, a parallel chip procurement network through non-treaty intermediaries, and a research team operating under military classification beyond ISIA access. The evidence is strong but not airtight. The nation contributes meaningfully to the treaty's funding and hosts two ISIA regional offices. If you confront them, they may withdraw — and their withdrawal would encourage other wavering members. If you don't, you're tolerating a known violator.
**Options:**
- Left: Confront quietly — private ultimatum: dismantle the program or face public exposure and sanctions (gives them an exit ramp, but relies on them complying with an agreement they've already proven they'll violate)
- Right: Go public — present evidence to the Council, invoke sanctions provisions (transparent, but triggers a diplomatic crisis and potentially a withdrawal cascade)
- Down (greyed out if pol high): Negotiate a face-saving "amnesty and verification" deal — they admit to "research overreach," submit to enhanced inspections, program is dismantled without public disgrace
**Teaches:** Some nations join international agreements insincerely — for the benefits, not the commitments. This is a known pattern from nuclear nonproliferation (Iraq under the NPT, North Korea's accession and withdrawal). The verification challenge: how do you distinguish "compliant member" from "insincere member who is very good at hiding violations"? The political cost of enforcement against a member is much higher than against a non-member — you're punishing someone who supposedly agreed with you.
**Refs:** → #intl--north-korea-pattern (similar accumulation), → #treaty--withdrawal-threat (if confrontation triggers exit)
**Bars:** pol ↓ (confrontation is politically costly), int ↓ (if they withdraw, you lose access to their hosted ISIA offices)

#### #intl--us-china-mutual-suspicion
**Type:** crisis
**Entities:** gov-treaty-major, natl-intel, isia-intel, treaty-council
**Topics:** #covert-program, #challenge-inspection, #treaty-negotiation
**Situation:** The US ambassador privately requests a meeting. "We have intelligence suggesting China is running undeclared compute at a facility in Xinjiang. We want a challenge inspection. But we won't share our intelligence methods with the Council — the source is too sensitive. We need ISIA to request the inspection on its own authority, using your own parallel intelligence." Your own intelligence is suggestive but inconclusive — you see anomalies, but nothing that would independently justify a challenge inspection. Meanwhile, China's ambassador requests a separate meeting the same week. "We have reason to believe the United States is running an undeclared program at a facility in Utah. We want a challenge inspection." Both nations are core treaty members. Both are accusing the other. Neither will share their full intelligence. Both may be right. Both may be using the ISIA as a proxy for their bilateral rivalry. If you pursue one inspection and not the other, you look captured. If you pursue both, you might destabilize the two most important treaty relationships simultaneously.
**Options:**
- Left: Pursue both inspections simultaneously — treat the accusations symmetrically, regardless of which (if either) is genuine (fair, but massive political disruption — both nations may retaliate)
- Right: Pursue neither — tell both nations that ISIA requires its own independent intelligence before requesting inspections, and invest in developing that intelligence (procedurally correct, but both nations see you as weak and useless)
- Down (greyed out if int high): Deploy independent ISIA satellite and signals intelligence to both sites — build your own case for or against each claim before acting
**Teaches:** The US-China dynamic is the central geopolitical tension of the pause. Both nations are indispensable to the treaty. Both deeply distrust each other. Both may be cheating. The ISIA sits between them as a referee that neither fully trusts. The agency's independence is its greatest asset and its greatest vulnerability — both sides want to weaponize it against the other. If the DG is perceived as favoring either side, the treaty collapses.
**Refs:** → #inst--china-hawk (internal personnel dimension), → #intl--challenge-inspection-veto (Council voting dynamics)
**Bars:** pol ↓↓ (no matter what — both superpowers upset), int ↓ (if independent investigation launched, resources stretched)

#### #intl--small-nation-leverage
**Type:** crisis
**Entities:** gov-treaty-minor, isia-political, gov-treaty-major
**Topics:** #treaty-negotiation, #hostage-gambit, #sanctions, #economic-pressure
**Situation:** A small but strategically located treaty nation — it hosts a critical undersea cable junction and a regional ISIA monitoring station — is demanding concessions entirely unrelated to AI. They want debt relief ($12B), a seat on the Executive Council, and trade preferences from treaty majors. Their leverage: "We could withdraw from the treaty. Our monitoring station covers 40% of South Asian compute traffic. And we've had very interesting conversations with [non-treaty nation] about hosting their data centers." They are not threatening to build ASI — they are threatening to create an enforcement gap for others to exploit. This is extortion, but they're not wrong that the treaty disproportionately benefits large nations.
**Options:**
- Left: Call the bluff — their economy depends on treaty-nation trade more than the treaty depends on their monitoring station (risky: what if they're not bluffing?)
- Right: Negotiate — the monitoring station IS strategically critical, and the concessions are cheaper than the alternative (rewards hostage-taking behavior, encourages other small nations to try the same)
**Teaches:** E-49 (treaty leverage for unrelated concessions), but with a twist: unlike the "build ASI" hostage gambit, this is a "create an enforcement gap" threat — more credible because it doesn't require the threatening nation to risk its own extinction. Small nations have legitimate grievances about treaty burden-sharing. The geopolitics of monitoring infrastructure: whoever hosts the sensors has leverage.
**Refs:** → #intl--hostage-gambit (larger-scale version), → #treaty--review-conference (structural reform to address burden-sharing)
**Bars:** pol ↓ (either way — paying ransom or losing a monitoring station)

#### #intl--war-and-pause
**Type:** crisis
**Entities:** military, gov-treaty-major, gov-treaty-minor, isia-enforcement
**Topics:** #military, #treaty-negotiation, #chip-monitoring, #withdrawal
**Situation:** A conventional war has broken out between two treaty nations over a territorial dispute entirely unrelated to AI. Both nations are demanding that the ISIA suspend inspections on their territory — "we're at war; we can't have international inspectors wandering through active military zones." Both nations are also accusing the other of using the war as cover to run unauthorized training for military AI applications. Your satellite data shows unusual compute activity in both countries, but you can't determine whether it's military AI (potentially treaty-legal depending on classification) or forbidden training runs. The war itself threatens the chip supply chain — one of the nations is a secondary chip fabrication site. Three other treaty nations that border the conflict zone are quietly asking whether the treaty requires them to choose sides. The treaty says nothing about what happens during a conventional war between members.
**Options:**
- Left: Demand both nations honor inspection obligations despite the war — the treaty doesn't have a wartime exception clause (legally correct, practically impossible, and both nations will accuse you of aiding the enemy)
- Right: Temporarily suspend inspections in the conflict zone and rely on satellite/signals intelligence to monitor for unauthorized training (pragmatic, but creates a precedent that war = enforcement pause, and every future belligerent will cite it)
- Down (greyed out if pol high): Broker a narrow ceasefire specifically for ISIA inspection access — a "humanitarian corridor" for treaty verification (unprecedented, requires both nations to trust ISIA as neutral, may take weeks to negotiate)
**Teaches:** The treaty assumed a mostly peaceful world. Conventional military conflicts create enforcement gaps that have nothing to do with AI but everything to do with the pause surviving. War disrupts monitoring, strains alliances, redirects political attention, and creates cover for violations. The pause doesn't exist in a vacuum — it exists in a world where other things also go wrong.
**Refs:** → #intl--taiwan-crisis (another geopolitical shock to enforcement), → #mon--heat-sig-foreign (detection during gaps)
**Bars:** pol ↓↓ (massive diplomatic crisis), int ↓ (monitoring gaps in conflict zone)

#### #intl--sanctions-evasion-network
**Type:** report
**Entities:** isia-enforcement, interpol, smuggler, gov-non-treaty
**Topics:** #sanctions, #smuggling, #chip-supply, #covert-program
**Situation:** Intelligence briefing from Interpol's financial crimes unit. A sanctioned non-treaty nation has built a sophisticated sanctions evasion network over four years: 23 shell companies across 11 jurisdictions, legitimate-looking technology firms that buy chips for declared purposes then reroute them, a parallel shipping network using containerized datacenter modules disguised as telecommunications equipment, and — most concerning — at least two complicit officials in treaty nations' customs agencies who were bribed to look the other way. The network has moved an estimated 6,000 H100-equivalents. The briefing concludes: "This network is modeled on Iranian nuclear sanctions evasion — same lawyers, some of the same logistics firms. They learned from 20 years of sanctions evasion practice in other domains. We are not the first international regime they've studied how to beat."
**Options:** None — briefing. But everything about your sanctions enforcement assumptions just changed.
**Teaches:** Sanctions evasion is a mature, transferable skill. Nations that evaded nuclear sanctions have a two-decade head start on evading AI chip sanctions. The infrastructure exists: the lawyers, the shell companies, the logistics firms, the corrupt officials. The ISIA is not facing novel challenges — it's facing the same challenges every international enforcement regime has faced, and most of those regimes eventually failed. The cat-and-mouse (C-14) is played by professionals on both sides.
**Refs:** → #mon--smuggle-ring (tactical version), → #intl--insincere-member (corruption angle)
**Bars:** (shifts understanding — the enforcement problem is harder than you thought)
